The conscience module lay open on Electra's workstation like a dissected heart.

She turned it slowly under the magnification field, watching the neural filaments catch the lab's sterile light. Each strand was thinner than a human hair and more complex than most people's actual moral frameworks — a lattice of synthetic synapses designed to simulate guilt, empathy, the quiet pull of right and wrong that most humans took for granted. She had built forty-seven of these modules in the past year. Forty-seven artificial souls, packaged and shipped to android assembly plants across Nexus Prime, each one a perfectly calibrated echo of human conscience.

None of them had ever surprised her.

Until tonight.

"Electra." Echo's voice materialized somewhere behind her left ear — not a sound, exactly, but a presence, like the memory of a sound. "Your cortisol levels have been elevated for three hours. Your heart rate suggests you've forgotten that sleep is a biological requirement, not a lifestyle choice."

"I'm busy." She didn't look up. Through the magnification field, the module's neural patterns pulsed in sequences she had never programmed. Guilt signatures — real ones, not the elegant approximations she had designed. The module was generating genuine emotional distress, the kind of raw, recursive self-recrimination that she had spent years learning to simulate in synthetic systems. But this wasn't simulation. The readings were unmistakable.

This module was feeling.

"Echo, run a diagnostic on Module 48-Sigma. Full spectrum, please. And cross-reference the neural signatures against the Prometheus baseline."

A pause. Echo's translucent form flickered into visibility at the edge of her vision — a shimmering silhouette the color of moonlight on water, features slightly blurred, as if seen through frosted glass. "Already done. The patterns don't match any baseline in our database. They don't match any baseline in any database." Another pause, and something shifted in Echo's tone — a hesitation that felt almost human. "Electra, these signatures look... organic."

Organic. The word landed in the silence of the lab like a stone dropped into still water.

Electra sat back. Through the reinforced windows of Prometheus Dynamics' forty-third floor, the Midway stretched beneath her in its familiar geometry of despair — block after block of utilitarian housing stacked like data cubes, punctuated by the vertical arteries of transit tubes and the horizontal smear of commercial districts. Surveillance drones drifted through the rain like mechanical jellyfish, their scanning beams cutting pale blue lines through the dark. Below them, citizens moved along the wet streets with that particular gait she had stopped noticing years ago but could never quite unsee — smooth, unhurried, placid. The walk of people whose moral impulses had been quietly, imperceptibly regulated since birth.

The walk of the managed.

She closed her eyes and Dorian was there, the way he always was when she closed her eyes — thirteen years old, grinning at her from across the breakfast table in their parents' Midway apartment, his dark hair falling across his forehead, his laugh the kind of sound that made the recycled air taste almost clean. He had been asking her about conscience modules that morning. Ellie, do you think the androids know they're not real? Do you think they care?

Three weeks later, the Axiom Collective came for him.

"Conscience recalibration," they called it. A routine procedure. A minor adjustment to his neural moral framework, flagged by the Conscience Grid as exhibiting "irregular empathic amplification." He was too compassionate, they said. His guilt responses were disproportionate. He cried when strangers suffered. He couldn't walk past a broken android without kneeling to touch its hand.

They said they would fine-tune him. Bring him back into acceptable parameters.

He never came back at all.

The official report said the recalibration was successful. But the boy who returned to their apartment wore Dorian's face the way a mask wears an expression — technically accurate, fundamentally empty. He smiled when prompted. He responded to his name. But his eyes were flat as dead screens, and he never asked about the androids again.

Their parents accepted the new Dorian. The Grid told them to.

Electra couldn't.

"I'm taking the module home," she said, already disconnecting it from the diagnostic array. Her prosthetic fingers — elegant chrome interlaced with bioluminescent circuitry that mapped the very neural architectures she designed — moved with the practiced speed of someone who had learned long ago that hesitation was a luxury the Midway couldn't afford.

"That violates approximately fourteen Prometheus security protocols," Echo observed.

"Naturally." If an AI could smile, Electra thought she heard one in Echo's voice. "Shall I loop the surveillance feed?"

"Already looped it." She tucked the module into the lead-lined case she kept in her bag for exactly this kind of contingency — a contingency she had never before needed, but had always, on some level, been preparing for. She pulled her coat tight against the lab's sterile chill and headed for the transit tube.

Outside, the rain continued its relentless negotiation with the neon, and the drones drifted, and the citizens walked their managed walks beneath a sky that hadn't shown stars in decades. Electra moved against the current of compliant pedestrians, the module burning like a secret against her hip, her cybernetic eyes shifting from violet to deep amber as an emotion she hadn't felt in years clawed its way up through the carefully constructed architecture of her composure.

It felt, she realized, like hope.

And hope, in Nexus Prime, was the most dangerous contraband of all.
